The Geography of Fine Taste

2026-06-28 · Regional luxury and the terroir of indulgence

Luxury in food is always local before it is global. Saffron tagliolini with white truffle speaks of Piedmont and Emilia in a single forkful—the egg pasta of one region married to the earthiest ingredient of another, both available only in narrow seasons and at considerable cost.

Travel writing about food often maps these connections: which landscapes produce which delicacies, and how regional pride shapes what counts as fine dining. The Mediterranean loggia, the mountain butter, the summer heat pressing down—all of these situate the dish in a particular geography of taste.

Writers featured in Condé Nast Traveller Features trace such journeys regularly, linking destination, ingredient, and the rituals of the table. The result is a portrait of cuisine as landscape—flavor as a form of place.

To eat this way is to accept that some pleasures are tied to where and when. The truffle does not travel well in spirit, even when it travels well in fact. Part of the taste is knowing where you are.
